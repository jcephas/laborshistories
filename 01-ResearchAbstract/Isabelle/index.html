Xinjiang Cotton


Land and Labor in the Margins of Chinese Empire



Isabelle A. Tan
March 29, 2023
ARC 550

While present-day issues around “Xinjiang cotton” are well-documented, its history has been far less examined. Drawing on recent scholarship, census material, state media, and digital approaches to inaccessible sources, this project explores the early work of the Xinjiang Production and Construction Corps (XPCC) in the Manas River Valley during the “high socialist” Mao period. Reading along and against propagandistic sources, I will trace the shifting relations constituting Xinjiang’s cotton landscapes and labor. This research will unfold on the XPCC’s military reclamation farms (junken nongchang). In these scenes, I will ask: how was this environment a concrete means through which labor was constructed and imagined? Who constituted this labor and how was it represented? Settler logics of Sinification offer a critical framework for examining state violence at the margins of Chinese empire. However, this project aims to complicate narratives of the “model” (Han) worker, against whom the (Uyghur, Kazakh, and Turkic) “ethnic minority” is generally pitted. By situating subjectivity in the spaces of Xinjiang’s cotton production, this project attends to the embodied life between working bodies, the valorization of their difference, and their seemingly universal representation.
