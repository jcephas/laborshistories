The readings present a relational framework between the societal constructs of gender and labor performativity, both of which emanate (in parallel? perpendicularly?) from the human body. The former, dealt with most directly by Baron and Boris, reminds us that one has—but also is—a body. Bodies provide the only interface we have with the world (for now), and as our sole corporeal vehicle, we are dependent on them for survival, and for all the work required to survive, including the maintenance of said body. The thing is, every body is different, so laboring bodies endure and thus eventually behave differently. A dependence on the body disproportionally affects some people more than others, complicating labor-centric ideologies. Those who cannot labor are erased from such narratives. Slavishak depicts an historic manifestation of how the “delicately built” body of a woman labored with “nervous speed,” burning out faster than the body of a man, in turn producing a sideline that favored gendered roles of male labor and female domesticity. Kuskey’s text embodies this wrought of labor vividly in the story of Mary Richards—whose horrors will likely sit with me forever.1

In his earlier chapter, Slavishak shifts his 19th-century Pittsburgh-based analysis from the performance of sexed bodies to that of labor itself. The branding of the city through the exaltation of the muscular body of the male artisan privileges the spectacle of labor’s performance over its product. Mauss’ amusing encyclopedia of bodily performances, closer in time to Slavishak’s subjects than to Slavishak, allows Slavishak’s point to be embodied within another text once again. The relation between the texts, as well as the relation between the various forms of performance discussed within them and in my text above, point to a performative understanding over one based entirely in labor. Labor produces, but it can also be a product. Its performance matters not only in terms of efficiency and learnability/accessibility. It can become a performance in itself, and in doing so transforms processes of labor and work into acts of, well, acting. The laborer is in a play, and in turn is playing. The lens of performance thus lends a useful perspective for my research interest in the role of play within productive processes, and how systems can incorporate otherwise frivolous elements that might typically detract or be seen as distractions in productively exploitative ways. I began with a parenthetical about the relation of the trajectories of gender and labor performativity, and finally I come full circle: they are tangential. Productive processes eventually incorporate tangential vectors like play into their mechanisms.
