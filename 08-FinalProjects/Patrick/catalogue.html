Background

In 1912 and 1924, the Philippine Bureau of Corrections released prison commodity catalogues detailing the types of furniture and other miscellaneous products crafted from Philippine raw materials and incarcerated labor. The full catalogues of hundreds of items, each with specific descriptions as to materials, shipping considerations, dimensions, and price, are made freely available as PDF documents below. While my primary focus is on the wicker and hardwood furnitures created by skilled incarcerated craftsmen and put up for sale by the Bureau of Corrections, other commodities and services such as silverware (including collar buttons, cuff buttons, shirt-waist pins, belt buckles, napkin rings, cutlery, cane heads, canes, and “swagger sticks”) push-/pull-cart vehicles, and laundry services were advertised in the catalogues.

Victoria, concha. Materials: "the very best native woods...rubber tires...springs, all elliptic." Catalogue. Sales Rooms at Bilibid Prison, Manila, P. I. Bureau of Prisons, Industrial Division, (Manila: Bureau of Printing, 1912).

However, by 1924, the primary emphases of the catalogue had become 1. a full articulation of the carceral system in the Philippines, seemingly necessary to clarify for any potential shoppers, and 2. a catalogue of wicker and hardware furniture production in the Bilibid Industrial division, which had become the more profitable result of incarcerated labor (the Prison and Industrial Divisions in 1926 had together garnered 519,280.40 pesos, almost enough to cover the estimated costs of the carceral system of 552,857.27 pesos).1 The prison industrial divisions were thus crucial to the ongoing maintenance of the Philippine carceral system, then administered by the American civil government. In an effort to provide opportunities for further research into the wicker and hardwood furniture industry—much of which was concentrated in the Bilibid Prison during the American civil government and in New Bilibid Prison into the Commonwealth and independent governments—an organized selection of furniture made of wicker (rattan, but also bamboo, sea-grass, and hemp) and hardwood (primarily narra and acle) is presented below.
