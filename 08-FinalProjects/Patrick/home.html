The Bilibid Workshop

Between 1899 and 1935, rattan furniture saw an enormous surge in interest due to the Western bourgeois obsession with furnishing summer homes; wicker offered a cool and breathable form of furniture that exploded in popularity.1 Wicker chairs also acted as “photographers’ chairs,” a key addition to photographers’ studios throughout the 20th century.2 Today, vintage narra hardwood tables still auction for tens of thousands of pesos, while the prison’s famous and more reproducible peacock chair has now found its way through social media channels to inspire bespoke interior decor and $1,000+ Anthropologie items. While popular articles, videos, and posts across the internet—from Vox Media to TikTok—have hosted a resurgence of interest in these items, the harvesting, production, and marketing of them as Philippine forest products have been historically overlooked. This is perhaps due to the complex histories of incarceration, industrial production, and colonial oppression at the root of early rattan and narra furniture production, whose largest manufacturing plant in the Philippines was the Bilibid Prison Workshop.

The “Bilibid Workshop” became a brand name for the Bureau of Corrections’ (BuCor) perhaps most-well known industrial department—Department D—in Bilibid Prison, a panopticon built by Spanish Royal Decree in 1865 and reorganized to be more “sanitary” and efficient by the American colonial regime by 1901. Under the American military and civil governments, Bilibid Prison became a site of valorization of both incarcerated “criminals”—a number of whom were in fact political prisoners whose anticolonial resistance were met with state violence—and of the slew of wood and vine products harvested from across the heavily forested islands. Laborers incarcerated in Bilibid were given the choice to labor in several departments, but the production of furniture objects made of rattan, narra, and other vines and hardwoods in particular required skilled incarcerated craftsmanship.

While labor forced upon incarcerated people has a long historical trajectory, the incredibly detailed and skilled craftsmanship present in the Bilibid workshop seem to find precedent only in colonial contexts.3 As Anand A. Yang has recently posited, the establishment of the “prison handicraft complex” in British colonial Bengal saw a turn in the 1830s from extramural labor (such as on public works) to intramural labor; still this kind of labor was politically questioned and has not enjoyed the kind of cultural import that the Bilibid workshop products have had, at least in the United States.4 Furthermore, while the kind of historical research necessary in order to make conclusions about the trajectory of incarcerated handicraft labor in the United States from the twentieth century on is outside the scope of this research project, I hope to foreground further research into American-Philippine policy trajectories regarding prisons and incarcerated people. I would like to suggest potential relationships between the American colonial policy in the Philippines and the evolving current American incarceration model, in which incarcerated individuals in institutions like Maine State Prison are paid $1 to $2 per hour for handicraft that is marketed, sold, and profited from by the carceral state.

By considering the Bilibid Prison Workshop as a form of incarcerated labor introduced and operated by the increasingly efficient American colonial state, I hope to create an archive of colonial forestry, incarceration, craft commodity production, and carceral salesmanship in order to invite further research into the role of colonial prisons in reproducing idealized forms of social governance and political economy. This digital research project thus tracks modes of furniture production in early configurations of the Philippine prison system, from the harvesting of rattan and narra in the Philippines, to the materials’ processing in the sawmills at the Iwahig Penal Colony and San Ramon Prison and Penal Farm as useable material, to the fabrication of desirable pieces of furniture using incarcerated labor, and ultimately to the furniture’s marketing to national and international buyers. By illuminating these various modes of commodity production through design and archival work, I aim to draw attention to both the complex history of early 20th century furniture production, as well as the political and economic project of incarceration in a colonized setting.
