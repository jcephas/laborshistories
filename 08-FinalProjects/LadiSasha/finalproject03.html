Mapping Theaters and Cinema as Black Public Space

The three maps developed to visualize the dataset reflect the increase in theater sites over time. The first map illustrates all the theaters within the dataset with confirmed locations (Figure 3.1). The second map (Figure 3.2) shows the eight theater sites that have been identified between the years 1890 to 1909. Finally, the third map (Figure 3.3) layers the additional eight theater sites that have been located as active sites, opened and operated, between the years of 1910 and 1930. With further research to identify additional sites alongside expanding the parameters, Black theater history reflects a high communal capacity for building and institutional formation during this period. A few areas of growth for the dataset are to locate theaters outside of the city centers, to map the theaters situated in the landscape of historically Black universities and colleges, and to chart other spatial vernacular typologies where modes of Black play and performance occurred.

Questions on Architectural Play

Black spatial play is not only the theoretical framework of the project; it also speaks to the subjective orientation around play. On the subjective sensibility around play, Cadoo also writes,

“Like their white working-class counterparts, African Americans created vibrant leisure practices and institutions in their new cities of settlement. Wage-earning urbanites, excluded from many white venues in the Jim Crow city, organized clubs and joined institutions to derive as much enjoyment from their leisure time as possible. These collective black experiences played a critical role in mediating the urban experience of black folk. …African Americans gave meaning to their lives as free people, wageworkers, and city dwellers by claiming their “God given right to play.” The widespread popularity of cinema as a black urban amusement contributed to the social bonds, perspectives, and sensibilities of an emerging modern black public.” [6]

Many questions will continue to research in its subsequent stages: What are the differing contextual politics impacting Black urban and architectural development from 1890 to 1920? Due to the labor history surrounding Black performance workers, can we situate the Black theater as a site of differential spatial production to a racialized industry? Simultaneously, how can we continue to trace it as a part of a larger cultural project shaping Black built environments? What can we glean from the pursuit of building toward play at the precipice of freedom, citizenship, and land access?
