Socialist Solidarity

The rhetoric of “Solidarity” in East Germany provides important context for the development of foreign relations with Mozambique and the experiences of Vertragsarbeiter in the GDR. Pictured here is a poster, likely printed shortly after Mozambique gained independence from Portugal in 1975.

As discussed here, East Germany had long pursued friendly diplomatic relations with post-colonial states. In the case of Mozambique, the governing SED party began funding FRELIMO, the socialist party fighting Portuguese colonial forces, as early as 1963. This support came after an important organization was founded in the GDR in 1960: the Solidaritätskomitee für Afrika (“solidarity committee for Africa”) was initially meant to provide aid to newly independent African states. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, the organization was known as the Afro-Asiatisches Solidaritätskomitee due in large to its non-military support for North Vietnam and the Viet Cong. With the 1973 coup d’etat in Chile and the ousting of the socialist Allende government, the Solidaritätskomitee expanded its global remit. [1]

The Solidaritätskomitee was not a formal organ of the SED, which meant that it was largely supported by East German citizens via fundraising. [2] By the 1970s the concept of “Solidarität” had become ubiquitous in GDR society: a rally cry around which East Germans could celebrate the benevolence of their government, or an empty platitude easily reproduced on posters like the one above? As Hans-Georg Schleicher has argued, the development of a solidarity organization with a latent objective of legitimizing the East German state can be described as politically opportunistic, but it was also a legitimate source of material and financial support. [3]
