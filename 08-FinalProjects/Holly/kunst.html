Mozambique on Display

In keeping with the details of the Treaty of Friendship and Cooperationsigned in February 1979, Mozambican artwork was occasionally exhibited in the GDR. This newspaper clipping advertises a 1982 exhibit of “paintings and handicrafts” (Malerei und Kunsthandwerk) in the gallery at the TV Tower in Berlin, an exhibition space in the base of one of the best-known symbols of socialist infrastructure in East Berlin.

It's important to note that the artists exhibited here were not Vertragsarbeiter; rather, they were professional artists primarily based in Mozambique, including the painter and poet Malagatana Valente Ngwenya (often called Malagatana) and the sculptor Diaz Machate. Malagatana was celebrated as a prominent affiliate of FRELIMO before and during the Mozambican War of Indepence, and was named to East Germany’s Academy of Arts, the GDR’s highest recognition possible for an artist [1]. Machate, though less well-known, went on to study at the Dresden Kunsthochschule between 1983 and 1989. Malagatana reportedly discouraged the younger Machate from attending art school in East Germany because of the rigid and conservative nature of the curriculum [2].

We do not know if many Vertragsarbeiter had the opportunity to visit the exhibition, but it presents an opportunity to consider the role of foreign artwork in late socialist East Germany, as well as the different ways in which “Friendship and Cooperation” could manifest. At the same time that the GDR celebrated artists from Mozambique, its economy also depended on a workforce from the same nation. Socialist “solidarity” meant many things, and just as Mozambican laborers were largely on the margins of East German society, artwork from their home country was proudly exhibited in virtually the center of the GDR.
