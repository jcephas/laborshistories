The Kiss: Reunification and Separation

While many Vertragsarbeiter in the GDR experienced exclusion and separation from East German society, this was not always the case. The image above shows João Amido, left, with his partner Cornelia Itgmi Wandja, right. João came to the GDR as a Vertragsarbeiter in the 1980s, where he worked at the ball bearing factory “Josef Orlopp.” He met Cornelia at a bar in Berlin in early 1989, and they began dating. Cornelia was profiled by the German news publication Der Spiegel in November 2021, where she described their relationship: João moved into her apartment in summer 1989, and quickly became a father figure to her children. Both celebrated the Fall of the Berlin Wall on November 9, 1989, unaware that it spelled the end of their relationship. The photograph above was taken in November or December 1989, when Cornelia was “seven or eight months pregnant” with their son, Johannes.

Johannes was born in January 1990, while João was still employed at the ball bearing factory. Reunification measures included the privatization of much of the GDR’s economy, and the “opening” of the GDR to the West was devastating for most East German factories. Vertragsarbeiter were the first employees to be let go, and João lost his job in October 1990. Vertragsarbeiter were reportedly promised 3,000 DM to return to their countries of origin rather than stay in newly reunited Germany, and João was told he would need to return to Mozambique in order to formally start a life with Cornelia in Berlin. He left, and the couple lost touch soon after his departure.
