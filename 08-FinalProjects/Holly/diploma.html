Juma Madeira's diploma certifying his completion of Facharbeiter ("skilled worker") training at the VEB Motorradwerk Zschopau.

An important component of the Vertragsarbeiter agreement between the GDR and Mozambique was the training of skilled laborers. Above is a diploma earned by Juma Madeira as a "cutter" (Fr√§ser) during his employment at the VEB Motorradwerk Zschopau.

Juma is an important figure in the recent book by Marcia Schenck, and his experiences in both the GDR and upon his return to Mozambique make him a near-ideal case study in understanding Vertragsarbetier experience. Juma received the diploma above in 1986, five years after his arrival in the GDR. He completed a four-year contract before signing on for another four years at the VEB Motorradwerk Zschopau, during which he received his certification handling machinery. Although Juma took German, his diploma was also issued to him in Portugeuse.

This diploma allows us to scrutinize the concept of "legitimacy" latent in the Vertragsarbeiter program. A diploma issued in two languages was clearly meant to be legible to both East Germans and Mozambicans. The ideal of the program, though rarely realized due to economic conditions in Mozambique, was that Vertragsarbeiter would use specialized skills once they returned home at the end of their contract. But beyond the ideal practical dimensions of such certification, this paperwork is, ultimately, just that: a piece of paper, which ultimately could not change the economic realities of 1980s Mozambique.

Just as the Vertragsarbeiter program helped to legitimize the late East German state, so too did the idea of "certification" or "skill" reinforce its legitimacy as a "benevolent" program, while simultaneously benefitting the East German economy.

Image citation: Marcia C. Schenck, Remembering African Labor Migration to the Second World: Socialist Mobilities Between Angola, Mozambique, and East Germany. London: Palgrave Macmillan, 2023, p.116.
