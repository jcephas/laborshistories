Spaces of Labor,
Spaces of Life

(l) Erich Honecker greets Mozambican citizens in Maputo, February 1979. (r) Honecker and Mozambican president Samora Machel sign the Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation, Feb. 24, 1979.

Sourcing labor from sub-Saharan Africa was a continuation of policies that the GDR had developed shortly after its founding in 1949. In the early Cold War, East Germany (and, in different ways, other COMECON states) viewed diplomacy with post-colonial states as an important means of establishing itself as a German nation distinct from the Federal Republic (FRG): these foreign relations initially had the goal of “systematic exposure and denunciation of the peace-endangering, revanchist, and neo-colonial policies of the West German state.” [1] The GDR aimed to win allies by framing its founding as the result of a struggle similar to liberation movements: where East Germany had emerged out of the socialist struggle against fascism, postcolonial states were emergent out of a struggle against imperialism.

The GDR could not compete with the FRG in terms of financial assistance to “developing” countries, but it was able to supply those states with a specialized knowledge base and technical assistance, crucial to the development of new economies in newly independent nations. East German experts in a range of fields including engineering, agriculture, and industrial development traveled to various African countries as early as 1960, and in 1963, the GDR began to support FRELIMO by shipping products manufactured in East Germany like bicycles, typewriters, and medical equipment to its headquarters in Dar es Salaam, Tanzania. [2]

Following an almost 10 year-long war, Mozambique gained independence from Portugal in 1975 and FRELIMO became its single governing party. Facing high unemployment in the immediate aftermath of the war, Mozambique requested a means of sending citizens to the GDR for short-term labor and vocational training. [3] In February 1979 leaders from the GDR and Mozambique signed a “Treaty of Friendship and Cooperation” in Maputo, which formalized the relationship between the two states. Shortly after the signing of the treaty, the two nations agreed to a Vertragsarbeiter (contract laborer) agreement which stipulated that thousands of Mozambicans would be placed in East German factories. [6]

While the exchange of labor—specialized East German expertise for a usually untrained Mozambican industrial workforce—was a major component of the relationship between the two states, the GDR never lost sight of the origins of its foreign relations policies: maintaining a prominent and publicizable relationship with FRELIMO leaders and the Mozambican people remained a paramount priority of the SED party, which in turn reinforced the GDR’s self-fashioned image as a benevolent anti-imperial state.
