Imaging Vertragsarbeiter Labor
Fig. 1 (l): Workers at VEB Braunkohlenwerk Welzow. Fig. 2 (r): Workers in a chemical textile manufacturing.

Most, if not all, of the images taken here were for publicity puropses. The GDR was aware that showcasing a diverse workforce reflected positively on its Solidarität objectives: simultaneously legitimizing the state and completing laudable aid work in a variety of states. As an important means of showcasing socialist solidarity, images taken of Vertragsarbeiter generally give a positive impression of experiences of labor in East Germany: in Fig. 1, for example, a worker at the Welzow lignite open-pit mine is pictured with an East German colleague. From their posture and positions, we are led to believe that the East German is in a position of power or teaching, while the Mozambican, with an expression of contentment or curiosity, looks on. In Fig 2. we similarly see a Mozambican laborer, this time at a chemical fiber manufacturing center. Here the dynamic is not as clear: both employees appear fixated on their work, and the Mozambican seems to inspect the fiber being produced.

Fig. 3 (l): A mill. Fig. 4 (r): Trainees at the VEB Fischfang Rostock

We see similarities repeated in these two images. In the image on the left, both figures appear absorbed by their work, but their expressions do not allow us to view this relationship as congenial—-at least not easily. On the right, Mozambicans train at the industrial fishery VEB Fischfang Rostock learn logistics. Images like these four replicate the dual nature of the East German Vertragsarbeiter program: at once providing opportunities and setting up a distinction between German and Mozambican laborers. We might recall Cedric Robinson's argument in the first chapter of Black Marxism: difference, and not homogenization, was a constituent construct in the development of capitalism. [1] Thinking of the exploitation of difference in a socialist economy, as fraught as it may have been by the 1980s, might allow us to expand Robinson's thesis in new directions.

Fig. 5 (l): Two Mozambican workers at the Zschopau motorcycle factory. Fig. 6 (r): A group pushes a "Trabi," the well-known car manufactured in the GDR, out of the snow. The original image caption does not name a place, but the license plate code suggests the photo was taken near Karl-Marx-Stadt (Chemnitz), Saxnoy.


Of course, Vertragsarbeiter experiences were not always determined by forced notions of difference or subordination. There were opportunities for socializing and perhaps building community. There were also moments which must have been new and challenging.

Fig. 7: Workers socialize on an excursion to Königstein, a historic town in Saxony with a famous fortress, in 1988. A rare document of Mozambican and East German laborers together outside of working hours.
