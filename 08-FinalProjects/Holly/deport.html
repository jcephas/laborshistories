Forced Deportation

If a Vertragsarbeiter was found to be pregnant, they were subject to immediate deportation. The document above was written by a gynecologist who had determined that a patient, a worker at a garment factory in Großschonau, Saxony, was pregnant. In the text of the letter, the doctor recommends her patient for “immediate repatriation” (sofortige Rückführung). It is notable that this letter was written on paper with the company letterhead on it: the exam was probably carried out in the Vertragsarbeiter’s accommodation (probably a dorm-style worker’s hostel) and was likely in close contact with her supervisors at the VEB Frottana Großschonau.

While East German rhetoric proudly proclaimed equality between sexes, women Vertragsarbeiter were expected to make arrangements for childcare and other family-related matters before leaving Mozambique for the GDR. [1] Vertragsarbeiter were not allowed to bring their children to East Germany, and so a less-explored aspect of the Vertragsarbeiter program was its mandate of separating families. This is important to consider in light of the special emphasis the arrangements between East Germany and Mozambique put on education: while initiatives like the Schule der Freundschaft in Staßfurt were proud examples of the GDR’s efforts to educate a new generation of socialist students, many children of Vertragsarbeiter did not have the same opportunity.

By policing whether Vertragsarbeiter could work while pregnant and by barring parents from bringing their children, the GDR was effectively policing the ways in which laborers could use their bodies, and mandating that they rely on a network of community care. Future research could investigate the Vertragsarbeiter program through a Social Reproduction Theory (SRT) lens.
