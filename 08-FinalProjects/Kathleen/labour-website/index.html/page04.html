(Counter-)teleologies of labour

In Kotlovan, labour does not make men, but rather unmakes them. Not only are the foundation pit workers portrayed as constantly on the verge of exhaustion, 18 but, through the imperative to collectivise, they are juxtaposed to animals in such a way that suggests they are required to become animals. For example, the collectivised horses are portrayed as superior horses to uncollectivised horses, but, in this, render superfluous the men’s labour in their upkeep: «Напившись в норму, лошади вошли в воду и постояли в ней некоторое время для своей чистоты, а затем выбрались на береговую сушь и тронулись обратно[…] и тогда обобществленный скот стал вокруг и начал медленно есть, организованно смирившись без заботы человека» (‘Having drunk up their quota, the horses walked into the water and stood there a while for their cleanliness, and then they got out onto the dry shore and started on their way back[…] then the collectivised cattle stood around and began to eat slowly, having settled down methodically without the care of a person’, 238). The horses drink to their quota, maintain their own hygiene and act as one all without the men intervening. The digger Voshchev is driven to panic by the «душевное спокойствие жующего скота» (‘the spiritual peace of the chewing cattle’, 238). It seems that the animals «с точностью убедились в колхозном смысле жизни» while «он один живет и мучается хуже лошади» (‘had scrupulously convinced themselves of the kolkhoz meaning of life[…while] he alone lived and suffered worse than the horses’, 238). This anxiety finds another vehicle for expression in the character of the bear-blacksmith who helps the diggers to sniff out the kulaks in the village. When the workers first encounter the bear-blacksmith, he is working so zealously «что пахло паленой шерстью, сгорающей от искр металла, а медведь этого не чувствовал» (‘that it smelled of singed fur, burned by sparks off the metal, and the bear did not feel it’, 253). The bear is eager to work for the Socialist cause because he has been abused by (human) capitalists. 19 On the one hand, the bear may be interpreted as a symbol for the revolutionarily impassioned proletariat. On the other hand, his animality is fundamental to his efficiency as a worker: his labour is motivated by his oppression at the hands of humans. If the best worker is the most oppressed one, then it follows that the best worker is not a human.

This framing of productive, collectivised labourers as animals is carried forward into the dekulakisation and collectivisation of the village. After this violent day, 20 the digger Chiklin watches the village celebrate their collectivisation and the elimination of the kulaks:

The collectivised workers’ need to forget today in order to face tomorrow (in order to labour together as a collective farm) recalls Friedrich Nietzsche’s distinction between man (historical life) and beast (unhistorical life): 21

Hence, Chiklin unknowingly and ironically stages the confession that, in order to work towards the Socialist future, men must become animals bound to the present. 22 This creates a paradox, by which the historical pursuit of the Socialist utopia through labour is set at odds with the unhistorical labourers who seek to enact it; labour does not improve men dialectically but imposes the loss of their humanity.
