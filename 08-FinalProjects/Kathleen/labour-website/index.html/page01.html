Transitioning from Marxist teleology to Stalinist ontology

In Capital (1887), Karl Marx depicts labour as man’s metabolic intervention in the natural world. 1 Such an intervention not only transforms man’s environment but also necessarily transforms man himself. 2 The Hegelian idealist dialectic of self-movement and self-estrangement is re-interpreted by Marx through labour: 3 through labour man acts on nature and this labour also acts on him, initiating a teleology by which the worker is produced through his labour. 4 Yet, as a result of the alienation and stratification of labour under capitalism, 5 we can suggest that the worker produced by that labour is not homogeneous but depends on the type of labour completed by the worker. 6

In the early Soviet period, attempts begin to be made to eliminate the capitalist stratification of labour. Key among these is Lev Trotskii’s Literature and Revolution (1923), in which he argues for the integration of the intellectual labour of artists into the physical labour of workers. Trotskii asserts that, in the post-revolutionary period, the labour of art (once allied to the bourgeois classes) has remained largely separate from physical labour (the labour of the proletariat on which the revolution itself was founded). As such, Trotskii identifies art as part of the socioeconomic superstructure rather than the base and asserts that the ‘one of the final tasks of the revolution’ must be the creation of a new art which will overcome the division of intellectual and physical labour. 7 This, Trotskii predicts, will lead to the formation of a new type of man. 8

With Iosif Stalin’s ascent to power, Trotskii’s exile and the advent of collectivisation, the delineation of intellectual labour from physical labour and of superstructure from base capitulated to the ontologisation of language as reality. While outwardly maintaining a commitment to historical determinism, Stalinist discourse sought to pre-empt the dialectics of labour by attempting to operate a causal, ontological relationship between what is and what is said. Such a radical shift in language flies in the face of Saussurean semiotics, not presupposing a referential relationship between signifier (expression) and signified (content), 9 but that the signifier create the signified. Indeed, Jacques Lacan notes, for Stalin, ‘language is not a superstructure’. 10 Such a linguistic upheaval fractures what might be called the language-reality (reality as we symbolically perceive and therefore experience it) and the labour-reality (reality as it materially conditions the making of men through empirical absolutes: life, death, sickness, health).

The Stalinist discursive regime of state socialism attempted to ontologise its own discursive statements as lived reality; it attempted to make language-reality labour-reality. Of course, this is not to say that, when Iosif Stalin proclaimed at the height of the purges in 1935 that «Жить стало лучшее, жить стало веселее» (‘Life has got better, life has got happier’), that this constatation made its contents true but rather that, in its utterance, it was designed to be received as such – as a language-reality. Evgenii Dobrenko has argued that Stalinist discourse (reproduced through Socialist Realist art) ‘created not “lies” but images of socialism that, through perception, return as reality – or, more specifically, as socialism’, arguing that people lived Socialism through Stalinist discourse. 11 Nonetheless, the political regime responsible for this discourse simultaneously produced an undeniable labour-reality (the most pertinent examples of this being the purges and the famines of 1930-1933), which could not support or even coincide with this discourse.
